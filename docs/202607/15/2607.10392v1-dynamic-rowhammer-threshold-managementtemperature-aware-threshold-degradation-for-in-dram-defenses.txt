# Dynamic Rowhammer Threshold Management: Temperature-Aware Threshold Degradation for In-DRAM Defenses ∗

## Aziz Alajmi Hoeseok Yang 

## Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Santa Clara University 

In-DRAM Rowhammer defenses pin the mitigation threshold at manufacture time, yet the true Rowhammer Threshold (TRHD) varies with runtime temperature. We propose Dynamic Rowham-mer Threshold Management , a defense-agnostic runtime layer that re-sources each defense’s threshold from the observed temper-ature once per epoch via a linear-T model with a VRD-motivated guardband g, projecting the result onto SALT-C, PRAC, and TRR through each defense’s threshold parameter. A decoupled oracle that scales physical TRHD per-DIMM by δ ∼ N (1 , σ ) breaks model self-consistency. The layer drives PRAC’s 72 staleness breaches at 85 ◦C to zero; at σ=0 .10 , sweeping g collapses PRAC breaches from 38.4 ( g=1 .0) to 9.6 ( g=0 .9). SALT-C drops from 10 nominal-static breaches to 2 (Dynamic) to 0 (bootstrap), at ≤5.1% latency. TRR is capacity-limited; the layer acts as a diagnostic. 

## 1. Introduction 

The Rowhammer vulnerability [1] has worsened with each DRAM generation: the Rowhammer Threshold (TRHD)—the minimum activations needed to induce a bit flip—has decreased from ∼139K in 2014 [1] to below 4.8K in modern DDR4 [2], and recent in-DRAM defenses already target sub-1K TRHD for DDR5 (e.g. SALT [3] provisions for TRHD as low as 500). In-DRAM defenses such as Target Row Refresh (TRR, vendor-proprietary; reverse-engineered model in [4]), Per-Row Activa-tion Counting (PRAC) [5], and SALT-C [3] mitigate this threat by tracking row activations and refreshing potential victims when a threshold is exceeded. However, the defenses we evaluate, and many pub-lished counter-based Rowhammer defenses, are parameterized around a fixed assumed TRHD value rather than recomput-ing that value from runtime temperature. This is problematic because the true TRHD varies strongly with runtime oper-ating conditions. Our linear regression on the aggregated SpyHammer [6] BER–temperature data across DDR4 chips from Micron, Hynix, and Nanya shows a near-linear T depen-dence with up to ∼3.3 × within-module BER span over the 50–95 ◦C operational range. VRD [7] further shows that TRHD fluctuates stochastically over time, with minima appearing only after tens of thousands of measurements. As the gap be-tween a defense’s configured threshold and the true (condition-dependent) TRHD widens, the defense becomes progressively less effective, creating an exploitable threshold staleness win-dow. A defense calibrated for 65 ◦C nominal operation silently under-mitigates when the system runs hot.

## 2. Background and Related Work 

Rowhammer defenses. Counter-based defenses track acti-vations and refresh potential victims when counts exceed a threshold derived from TRHD. TRR is a vendor-proprietary in-DRAM mechanism (no academic spec); we use the reverse-engineered model from Hassan et al. [4], in which a small per-bank top-K tracker drives victim refreshes piggybacked on REF. PRAC [5] (introduced in JESD79-5C as an optional feature) uses per-row counters with a threshold sized below TRHD by the standard’s ABO grace window. SALT-C [3] (the coordinated-refresh variant of SALT, distinct from the base SALT also introduced in [3]) operates at subarray granularity: each subarray has an Activation Counter (ACtr), coordinated refresh decrements counters during regular REF commands, and an Alert-Based Operation (ABO) fires when ACtr exceeds ATH = 2 × APM. All three defenses calibrate their thresholds to a fixed TRHD value at manufacture. 

Runtime TRHD variability and threshold staleness. 

SpyHammer [6] characterized 120 DDR4 chips across four ven-dors and reported a near-linear BER–temperature dependence over 50–95 ◦C for Micron, Hynix, and Nanya (we measure up to 

∼3.3 × within-module BER span on the aggregated data), and an inverted negative dependence for Samsung. Yaglikci et al. [8] 1arXiv:2607.10392v1 [cs.AR] 11 Jul 2026 measured TRHD vs. wordline voltage ( VP P ) on 272 DDR4 chips and found that TRHD increases monotonically with re-duced VP P : lower wordline drive weakens the inter-wordline coupling Rowhammer exploits, so VP P under-drive is a mit-igation knob rather than a staleness axis (we discuss this in §3.1). VRD [7] characterized 160 DDR4 chips and found that Rowhammer thresholds fluctuate stochastically over time, with worst-case thresholds only appearing after tens of thousands of measurements. A defense calibrated at 65 ◦C nominal there-fore operates with a stale threshold when the same DIMM runs hotter, silently under-mitigating; closing this thermal-staleness gap by recomputing TRHD from observable environment vari-ables at runtime is the goal of this paper. 

Related work. Prior temperature-aware Rowhammer work has been on the attack side: SpyHammer [6] uses tempera-ture as a side channel to amplify Rowhammer effectiveness; we invert it and use the same characterization data to drive a defense. Yaglikci et al. [8] characterize the VP P /TRHD re-lationship that motivates a future controller-side under-drive mitigation policy (§3.1); we do not exercise it in this paper. VRD [7] characterizes the stochastic component of the thresh-old and motivates our guardband. The strategy of recomputing a DRAM-management threshold from runtime observables has direct precedent in the refresh literature: RAIDR [9] groups rows by measured retention time and refreshes each group at its own rate, and AVATAR [10] additionally tracks variable retention time (VRT) flips and re-promotes rows on the fly. Both adapt a per-row retention threshold in response to ob-served behaviour; our method does the analogous thing for the per-bank Rowhammer threshold using T . Other counter-based defenses—BlockHammer [11], Mithril [12], Hydra [13]—are orthogonal: each exposes a per-bank or per-row threshold vari-able, so the same projection mechanism we describe in §3.3 extends to them with no additional model parameters. Non-counter defenses (e.g. row-swap families) are not addressed by this work and do not benefit from dynamic thresholding, since they have no threshold to project onto. Recent attack work (Blacksmith [14], ZenHammer [15]) demonstrates DDR4/DDR5 attack feasibility; our defense is orthogonal to attack pattern and is equally applicable. To our knowledge no prior in-DRAM Rowhammer defense recomputes its threshold at runtime from environmental observables. 

## 3. Proposed Method 

We propose Dynamic Rowhammer Threshold Management , a defense-agnostic runtime layer that re-sources each existing de-fense’s threshold from the live temperature T once per epoch, preserving the threshold-side assumptions of each defense where the defense’s tracking capacity and timing model are otherwise sufficient. The layer has three pieces: a degrada-tion model (§3.1), a per-epoch update procedure (§3.2), and a per-defense projection of the effective TRHD onto each de-fense’s threshold variable (§3.3). The threshold-write path is the one spec change the layer requires: today’s DDR5 standard does not expose per-bank PRAC/TRR threshold registers as host-writable, and vendor TRR thresholds are proprietary; our update assumes a future JEDEC addendum exposing them via a new mode-register class. We discuss the deployment impli-cations in §5 and treat this as an explicit limitation rather than a free retrofit. 

3.1. Dynamic TRHD Model 

The effective TRHD at epoch t is the product of two multiplica-tive factors, each ≤ 1:TRHD eff (t) = TRHD init · ftemp (T ) · g. (1) The multiplicative form composes naturally with published characterizations that report relative threshold change with T .Temperature is commonly observable at runtime via existing SPD-side sensors (TSE2004av on DDR4, SPD-Hub TS on DDR5), so the controller does not need to acquire new instrumentation to drive ftemp ; g is the only parameter not directly grounded in characterization data and is chosen empirically (§4.2). The SPD-side sensor reports a single DIMM-coarse temperature, so T is per-DIMM per-epoch; intra-DIMM thermal gradients (which can exceed the 20 ◦C span we sweep under burst workloads) are out of scope, and the projected TRHD eff is applied uniformly to every bank on the DIMM. 

Temperature ( ftemp ). SpyHammer [6] characterizes 120 DDR4 chips and reports near-linear BER vs. T over 50–95 ◦Cfor Micron/Hynix/Nanya parts. Inverting BER to TRHD within a hammer-count neighbourhood gives a linear local fit: 

ftemp (T ) = max( fmin , 1 − βT · (T − Tref )) (2) with Tref =338 K and βT = 1 .2 × 10 −2/K; our linear fit to [6]’s aggregated data yields R2=0 .89 ( [6] themselves report per-module polynomial fits rather than an aggregated linear one, so the R2 is our own aggregation, not theirs). At 85 ◦C, 

ftemp ≈ 0.76 , so TRHD eff ≈ 760 from TRHD init =1000 . Be-cause [6] reports BER rather than TRHD directly, we treat the BER-to-TRHD conversion as a local approximation around the evaluated hammer-count regime; this approximation is used only to parameterize the simulator, and on-silicon calibra-tion would be required before deployment. We flag a stronger caveat: the layer’s correctness rests on HC first (the activation count at which the first bit flips) being temperature-dependent, while SpyHammer measures BER at fixed HC. The BER-to-HC first link follows from the population statistics of disturb errors within a row, but to our knowledge no published char-acterization measures HC first vs. T directly. VRD [7] shows that HC first varies stochastically over time on a single device, which is consistent with our assumption that it is sensitive to underlying physical state, but is not a direct measurement of thermal dependence. Direct HC first -vs-T characterization is the most important calibration step before deployment, and is the load-bearing assumption a future on-silicon study could ei-ther validate or invalidate. We use linear rather than Arrhenius because an Arrhenius alternative would require an activation-energy Ea with no validated value for the wordline-coupling mechanism Rowhammer exploits—retention-failure Ea values do not transfer because the physical mechanism is gate-oxide 2leakage, not inter-wordline coupling. Within the 45 ◦C operat-ing window, a linear fit is faithful to [6]’s aggregated data, eval-uates in one multiply-subtract (§5), and is trivially invertible. We do not claim it generalizes outside 50–95 ◦C. SpyHammer’s Obs. 2 reports an inverted sign on Samsung; we accommodate this with a per-vendor βT dispatched at enrollment (§3.2). 

Wordline voltage ( VP P ) is a mitigation knob, not a stal-eness axis. Yaglikci et al. [8]’s VP P sweep on 272 DDR4 chips shows that reducing VP P raises HC first —lower wordline drive weakens the inter-wordline coupling Rowhammer exploits, so the cell’s TRHD margin improves rather than degrades. VP P is therefore not a staleness axis in the sense T is. A complemen-tary deployment that under-drives VP P when the controller detects elevated thermal pressure (trading slower wordline pull-down for additional TRHD headroom) composes with our dynamic threshold layer but introduces its own timing co-design questions; we leave that policy to future work and operate at nominal VP P throughout the evaluation. 

VRD guardband ( g). g is a multiplicative derate covering threshold variability not captured by ftemp . VRD [7] character-izes 160 DDR4 chips and shows that Rowhammer thresholds fluctuate stochastically over time, with worst-case thresholds only appearing after tens of thousands of measurements. The deterministic ftemp tracks only the population-mean response and cannot, by construction, account for per-row temporal vari-ation. A non-zero g is therefore necessary. Yaglikci et al. [8] ad-ditionally observe that 14–15% of rows show VP P -Rowhammer response opposite to the population mean, evidence of mea-sured per-row dispersion around any nominal voltage oper-ating point that motivates a non-trivial g independently of VRD’s temporal variation. We use a multiplicative rather than additive form because per-DIMM residuals around the popula-tion fit scale with TRHD itself: an additive guardband would over-protect at low TRHD (where 100 ACTs is a large fraction of the threshold) and under-protect at high TRHD. We choose 

g=0 .9 as the empirical Pareto knee from the sweep of §4.2; that sweep also shows the choice is not sensitive to ±0.05 on this trace. 

3.2. Per-Epoch Update 

At each epoch boundary the controller executes a four-step procedure, identical for every defense: 1. Sample : read T from the SPD-side TS device over SMBus on DDR4 (e.g. TSE2004av) or from the SPD-Hub thermal sensor on DDR5 (e.g. TI TMP139 over I3C). 2. Evaluate : compute TRHD eff via Eq. 1. 3. Project : map TRHD eff onto the defense’s threshold variable (§3.3). 4. Write : issue one MRW per bank using the same controller-side machinery that issues RFM commands. Steps (1)–(3) take a handful of nanoseconds; the cost is dom-inated by the per-bank writes (§5). We use a 10 ms epoch by default in deployment (1 ms in the simulator at DDR4-3200, i.e. ≈1.6 M cycles). On power-up the layer bootstraps with an environment-aware first-epoch threshold: it samples the SPD-side temperature sensor before any user traffic and seeds the defense at the steady-state projection for the observed T .§4.1 reports a measured run with this bootstrap policy showing SALT-C’s first-epoch transients drop from 2 to 0. 

Vendor dispatch. The Samsung sign-flip from §3.1 is han-dled with a one-byte lookup: at enrollment the controller reads the SPD MID (Manufacturer ID) byte and selects the appropri-ate βT (βT >0 for Micron/Hynix/Nanya, βT <0 fit to SpyHam-mer’s Samsung subset). The projection in §3.3 is unchanged. 

3.3. Per-Defense Threshold Projection 

The manager interacts with each defense only through that de-fense’s threshold parameter (published for SALT-C and PRAC; the threshold parameter in the reverse-engineered design for TRR); the projection is mechanically derived from the defense’s own security contract. Because TRHD eff ≤ TRHD init by con-struction, every projection below produces a threshold at most as loose as the nominal-static one—the manager only ever 

tightens mitigation, never relaxes it. 

SALT-C. SALT’s security analysis [3] bounds MaxACT =

ATH + ( B−1) APM + 25 with B bundles per subarray and ATH =2 APM. Setting MaxACT = 2 TRHD eff and solving: APM ← max( APM min , ⌊(2 TRHD eff − 25) /(B + 1) ⌋) (3) preserves SALT’s MaxACT bound at the new TRHD eff : ev-ery row is refreshed before its activations cross the degraded physical TRHD. 

PRAC. The per-row counter alerts before the row crosses TRHD: ATH PRAC ← max(1 , TRHD eff − NABO ), (4) where NABO is the ABO grace budget: the number of activa-tions the controller may complete between ALERT_n assertion and mitigation finishing. JESD79-5C [5] parameterizes this through tRFM, tABO , and bank-queue depth without quoting a closed-form upper bound; SALT [3] estimates a typical range of 4–30 ACTs for shipping DDR5 timings. The simulator we use does not model bank-queue accumulation during ABO, so we take the conservative limit NABO =4 . For TRHD init =1000 this gives nominal-static ATH=996; nominal-static, worst-static, and Dynamic are sized with the same NABO , so the qualitative result is preserved at any value in the cited range. We discuss this as a modeling limitation in §6. 

TRR. A blast-radius-style fixed-depth tracker uses the con-ventional half-TRHD threshold: ATH TRR ← max(1 , ⌊TRHD eff /2⌋). (5) TRR’s failure mode under our trace is tracker capacity, not threshold staleness (§4), so the projection is correct in form even when insufficient in effect. Adding a fourth defense requires only its threshold contract, not a model retrain. 

## 4. Evaluation 

We implement the Dynamic TRHD model in DRAMSim3 [16]. Our DDR4-3200 configuration uses 1 channel, 2 ranks, 16 3banks/rank, 65K rows/bank, 256 subarrays/bank, VP P =2.5 V. We set TRHD init =1000 to match the threshold range SALT-C [3] provisions for in DDR5 (the cited paper provisions for TRHD as low as 500); this choice is below recent measured DDR4 TRHDs ( ∼4.8K [2]) but matches the regime in which next-generation in-DRAM defenses are expected to operate. Absolute breach counts depend on this choice and a TRHD init 

sensitivity sweep is outside the scope of this paper. The threshold-side ordering—both worst-static and Dynamic pro-duce a threshold at most as loose as nominal-static (Eqs. 3–5)— is invariant in TRHD init . The resulting breach ordering between Dynamic and worst-static, however, depends on the defense: for PRAC, Dynamic matches or beats worst-static in Table 1; for SALT-C, Dynamic carries 2 first-epoch breaches at 85 ◦Cthat worst-static avoids by construction (the bootstrap variant of §4.1 eliminates them); for TRR, tracker capacity rather than threshold selection dominates, and small breach differences between calibrations do not reflect the threshold-side claim. All runs are driven by a double-sided Rowhammer trace (600K activations alternating between two aggressors 64 KB apart, one activation every 10 cycles) executed for 7M cycles. We implement three representative in-DRAM defenses span-ning the architectural design space—SALT-C [3] (subarray-aggregate with ABO bank-stall), TRR [4] (fixed-depth K=4 

top-K tracker, piggyback-on-REF; K=4 is at the low end of the 1–28 entries that TRRespass [17] and U-TRR [4] identify in shipping DDR4, and is the adversarial baseline at which capacity becomes the binding constraint), and PRAC [5] (per-row counter, piggyback-on-REF)—and drive all three with the identical trace. Evaluation proceeds in two parts: (i) a unified threshold-staleness attack where we operate each defense hot-ter than its calibration point and compare nominal-calibrated, worst-case-calibrated, and Dynamic configurations side-by-side (§4.1); and (ii) a latency-overhead deep-dive on SALT-C, the only defense whose mitigations stall the bank and therefore whose overhead is observable in the latency distribution (§4.2). 

Metrics. We report four counters throughout the evalua-tion. Breaches (the primary security metric) are rows whose oracle-counted activations crossed the true effective TRHD be-fore any mitigation fired for them—rows physically exploitable regardless of what the defense thought. Breaches are observed by an oracle side-channel attached to DRAMSim3 and are independent of the defense being measured. Zero breaches means the defense has always acted in time. Mitigations are the events the defense itself counts: ABO-triggered coordinated refreshes for SALT-C, and piggybacked neighbor-refreshes on REF for TRR/PRAC. Each mitigation resets the defense’s counter for the offending row, so PRAC/SALT-C can achieve zero breaches even at very low TRHD if they mitigate often enough—the cost is the ABO stall (SALT-C, bank-blocking) or the REF-time refresh (TRR/PRAC, piggybacked and there-fore latency-free). Evictions are specific to TRR’s fixed-depth tracker: when a newly-activated row would need a tracker slot and all K slots are occupied, the lowest-count entry is dis-placed. An evicted row’s count is lost, so future activations to it restart from zero—the mechanism by which an attacker can hide inside the tracker’s blind spot. PRAC and SALT-C have no eviction concept ( K = ∞ per-row, subarray-aggregated respectively). ABOs are the SALT-C-specific mitigation: an Alert-Based Operation stalls the bank for tRFM =350 ns while the controller refreshes victim rows. ABO count therefore doubles as SALT-C’s overhead metric. 

4.1. Threshold-Staleness Attack Across Three De-fenses 

Setup. For each defense we sweep three calibration strategies. The physical TRHD init (a DIMM-level property) is fixed at 1000; what differs is the value the defense believes when sizing its threshold. 

• Nom-static : defense sizes thresholds from TRHD =1000, the 65 ◦C calibration point. SALT-C APM=52 (Qureshi HPCA’26 Eq. 5 with B = 37 bundles [3]), PRAC threshold=996, TRR threshold=500. 

• Worst-static : defense sizes thresholds as if the DIMM always ran at 85 ◦C (TRHD =760). SALT-C APM=39, PRAC thresh-old=756, TRR threshold=380. This is the conservative static design choice available today. 

• Dynamic : thresholds recomputed per 1.6 M-cycle epoch from the current T via Eq. 1 with g=0 .9. At 65 ◦C: SALT-C APM=46. At 85 ◦C: APM=35. We sweep two environments: (a) 65 ◦C (calibration, TRHD =1000) and (b) 85 ◦C (thermal staleness, TRHD ≈760). 

VP P is held at nominal 2.5 V throughout; a VP P -driven mitigation policy is out of scope (§3.1). 

Decoupled oracle. A naive evaluation in which the oracle and the defense use the same ftemp fit is self-consistent by construction and cannot distinguish a correct mechanism from a tautology. To break that loop we draw a per-DIMM scalar

4.2. Latency Overhead 

In our simulator, TRR and PRAC mitigation refreshes are mod-eled as REF-piggybacked and add zero additional read latency beyond the baseline timing model (468.3 cy avg / 1,326 cy P99, identical to no-defense). We do not model PRAC’s in-trinsic counter-update timing overhead—e.g. the tRC increase (46 → 52 ns) discussed by SALT [3]—which would add a small uniform overhead to every ACT independently of mitigation events. SALT-C is the only defense that additionally stalls the bank on mitigation (ABO for tRFM =350 ns), so the latency ques-tion below is SALT-C-specific. Average read latency across the same trace as §4.1: no-defense 468.3, nominal-static 576.9, worst-static 606.7, Dynamic 584.7 / 606.2 at 65/85 ◦C. Dynamic costs +1.4% / +5.1% versus nominal-static (the +5.1% is the price of closing the 10-breach staleness gap nominal pays with correctness) and is 3.6% cheaper than worst-static at 65 ◦C / within 0.1% at 85 ◦C with identical breach counts. P99 tracks the average ( ∼3.0 ×), so the dynamic layer adds no tail outlier beyond the existing ABO stall. 

Guardband Pareto. g does meaningful work only once the oracle has variance. At σ=0 .10 (10 seeds) PRAC at 85 ◦Cshows a knee: 38.4 mean / 96 max breaches at g=1 .0, 24.6 / 99 at g=0 .95 , 9.6 mean / 24 max at g=0 .90 (75% drop in both mean and max), flat below 0.90 (residual is the worst-tail DIMM, δ=0 .928 ; the [0 .5, 1.5] clamp does not bind for any seed) while mitigation traffic rises monotonically. SALT-C’s Pareto is flatter (2 breaches across g ∈ [0 .7, 0.95] , 3.5 / 10 only at g=1 .0): APM-floor saturation buffers most per-DIMM variance, so smaller g mainly buys extra ABOs. We adopt g=0 .90 as the cross-defense default driven by the PRAC knee; this is a one-cell fit (one defense, environment, σ) and a production deployment would re-sweep on characterization data of its own fleet. 

## 5. Hardware Feasibility 

The four-step per-epoch procedure of §3.2 is dominated by the per-bank MRW writes; the numerical work is sub-nanosecond. 

Compute. The critical-path cost is dominated by the per-bank threshold writes, not the temperature read. Temperature is sampled asynchronously over SMBus/I3C from the SPD-side sensor and can be cached or pre-fetched by the memory controller once per epoch, so it does not need to sit on the critical path. The on-path work per epoch is one multiply-subtract for ftemp (T ) (<1 ns), one shift/subtract for the per-defense threshold, and one MRW per bank (32 banks × ∼ 8 ns 

≈ 260 ns), totaling ∼260 ns per 10 ms epoch—a 2.6 ×10 −5 duty cycle (∼0.003% of DRAM bandwidth). Sub-millisecond epochs remain below 0.3% bandwidth. 

Storage. For SALT-C at 256 subarrays × 32 banks × 2 ranks 

× 9-bit APM, the per-subarray threshold register file is ∼2.3 KB per channel. SALT-C’s own per-bank tracker is 0.48 KB/bank ×

32 banks × 2 ranks = 15 .4 KB per channel [3], so our register file is ∼6.7 × smaller. Per-bank TRR/PRAC thresholds need only ∼64 B per channel. Bandwidth: 32 MRW per 10 ms ≈

100 KB/s, six orders of magnitude below a DDR5 channel’s sustained ∼25 GB/s. 

Deployment dependencies. The layer requires (i) per-bank threshold mode-registers to be host-writable, which JESD79-5C does not currently expose for PRAC or TRR and which a future addendum would need to specify; and (ii) temperature 5readback via the SPD-side TS device on DDR4 (e.g. TSE2004av over SMBus) or via the SPD-Hub thermal sensor on DDR5 (e.g. TI TMP139 over I3C). Neither requires a new command class— only that the standard expose registers that vendors already implement internally for calibration. 

On-die alternative. The host-side path above is one of two deployment points; the other is an on-die implementation that avoids the JEDEC spec change entirely. DDR5 dies already contain a thermal sensor (used internally for refresh doubling above 85 ◦C), and per-bank PRAC/TRR thresholds are already DRAM-internal state. A small on-die ROM table that maps the sampled T to a derating factor, combined with a one-cycle multiplier on the existing per-bank threshold register, repro-duces the host-side projection without exposing any new host-writable mode-register or new sensor path. This is consistent with how vendors already implement other temperature-aware behaviors (refresh-doubling, MR4 temperature readback [5]) and may be a more practical adoption path: calibrating the tem-perature/threshold slope is a one-time fab-side task analogous to existing process-corner calibration. The trade-off is observ-ability: the host can no longer audit the projected threshold without a readback register. We do not implement the on-die variant in this paper because the simulator is host-driven, but the projection equations (§3.3) are unchanged. 

## 6. Conclusion 

Limitations. All results are simulation-only with a decou-pled oracle; on-silicon HC first -vs-T characterization is the most important next step (§3.1). The linear ftemp targets Mi-cron/Hynix/Nanya DDR4 (SPD-MID dispatch handles Sam-sung’s sign-flipped βT , but SPD MID is fragile on relabeled modules), and we do not claim the DDR4-derived fit extends unchanged to DDR5: process and circuit changes between gen-erations could shift βT or invert it in vendor-specific ways, and a deployment on a new generation should refit before relying on the fit. TRR is 2-of-3: the layer surfaces a capacity ceiling there that threshold tuning cannot reach. The trace covers ∼4epochs and one δ per DIMM at a single TRHD init =1000 ; multi-epoch thermal ramps, per-row VRT noise, and a TRHD init 

sweep remain future work. VP P as a controller-side mitigation knob (§3.1) is not exercised in this paper. The threshold-write path on the host-side deployment assumes a JEDEC addendum exposing per-bank threshold mode-registers that JESD79-5C does not currently define; the on-die alternative (§5) avoids this constraint. 

Adversarial assumptions. The contract that the layer “only ever tightens” the underlying defense’s threshold assumes the observed T is the actual cell temperature. An attacker who can manipulate the observable T (controlled cooling, co-tenant heating, or sensor spoofing in a multi-tenant deploy-ment) could push the defense into a relaxed regime; the on-die deployment with a die-internal sensor reduces but does not eliminate this attack surface. A separate epoch-quantization attack exists where the attacker hammers during a cool epoch and heats the bank between samples (e.g., by inducing activ-ity on neighbouring banks), causing the layer to size for the cool sample while the true cell temperature has already risen. Defenses include shortening the epoch under observed ∆T ,triggering an out-of-band T sample on ALERT_n, and adjust-ing g to absorb a per-epoch worst-case thermal excursion; we leave each to future work. Blacksmith [14] / ZenHammer [15]-style frequency-domain and multi-aggressor patterns are not exercised by our double-sided trace; we expect them to inter-act with the projection only through their effect on per-bank activation rate (which the layer does not currently track), so an attacker who maintains the same activation rate but a richer access pattern should see the same threshold-side response from the layer—we flag this as untested. Defense designers solve the hard problem; this paper handles the parameter that goes into their solutions. A thin runtime layer that re-sources TRHD from T once per epoch closes the staleness gap on PRAC (72 →0 at 85 ◦C), on SALT-C ( ≤5.1% latency, bootstrap to zero), and surfaces TRR’s capacity ceil-ing. The layer adds no per-defense model parameter, preserves the intended threshold contract for counter-based defenses, and composes with future counter-based defenses (BlockHam-mer [11], Mithril [12], Hydra [13]) through the same projection.
